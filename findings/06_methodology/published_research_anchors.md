## Anchor: Specific clinical-AI failure modes

| Study | Finding |
|---|---|
| MELD liver-transplant score | Female creatinine baselines are lower due to muscle mass; female patients are systematically under-scored → longer wait for transplant at equivalent severity (Webb et al., NPJ Digital Medicine 2025) |
| High-STEACS troponin trial | Sex-specific thresholds **→ 42% increase in identified MI in women vs 6% in men**, but treatment rates did not change (Chapman et al., BMC Medicine 2019) |
| REBOOT Heart Trial 2024 | Beta-blockers post-MI **increased mortality in women** but not men — reverses decades of standard-of-care (The Lancet 2024) |
| Delphi-2M foundation model | Endometriosis training data **~10× smaller** than diabetes despite similar female prevalence; 1-year accuracy: endo 63%, PCOS 69%, T2D 83%, HTN 78% (Nature 2025) |
| Cardiac-AI equal-representation study (Straw, Rees, Nachev 2024) | **13 of 16 experiments** showed higher female false-negative rates (range −3.4 to −17.8 pp) |
| Liver-disease AI (2022) | Random Forest: **21 pp** female false-negative disparity; Logistic Regression: **24 pp** |
| COVID-19 AI (Chung et al. 2021) | Male-trained model: accuracy dropped from 0.92 to 0.86 when applied to female patients |
| Google DeepMind AKI model | Female sensitivity **44.8%** vs male **56.0%** — training set was 94% male (Rajkomar et al., NPJ Digital Medicine 2018) |
| LSE Rickman 2025 (social care) | Google Gemma significantly downplays women's needs in case summaries; Llama-3 does not → bias is architecture-specific, fixable |
| JAMA Network Open 2023 (Kim et al.) | ChatGPT-4 recommended thrombolysis for White men only in one MI vignette; aggressive heart-failure therapy for men but not women, especially Hispanic women |
| FDA review of 692 cleared AI/ML devices | **3.6%** reported race/ethnicity; sex-stratified performance "almost never provided"; **~99%** omitted socioeconomic data |

## Anchor: The structural-design argument

*These are the framings from "The Price of Invisibility" that reframe the policy question:*

1. **Women are the stress test the system failed, and the blueprint for how it needs to evolve.** The demographic and longevity trends of the full population are moving toward the pattern women's health has always required — chronic, multi-system, longitudinal, context-dependent. Designing healthcare AI that works for women is designing the operating model for the next 50 years.

2. **The right reframe is not moral-claim but efficiency-claim.** Gender-based pricing in insurance has "no solid actuarial basis" — it is omitted-variable bias. Gender stands in for missing structural variables the models failed to capture.

3. **Women don't use more healthcare — the system makes them go through more steps to receive accurate care.** Higher utilization is structural inefficiency, not over-demand.

4. **The Functional Health Credit framework:** reframe Healthy Life Years as creditable assets. Early intervention becomes ROI-measured, not cost-avoided. This is the framework the full paper would rigorize.

## Use in the larger analysis

These anchor findings are the citation base for Steps 1–3 of the main analysis (population, bottleneck classification, scenario modeling). They substantially reduce the original-research burden: the work becomes *integration of existing published evidence + new empirical AI-behavior data*, not *generate the evidence from scratch*.

The specific translation points:
- The **11.3-pp clinician-accuracy drop** is the single most powerful motivating number for why observed-AI-deployment measurement matters — the AI-behavior pilots in this portfolio produce the direct input to that concern.
- The **$1T WEF/McKinsey number** is the scale anchor. The main scenario ranges (e.g., $100–300B annual for AI-addressable US recovery) are defensible fractions of that total.
- The **40% OECD ROI gap** is the system-efficiency anchor. The payer-structure comparison (CA / AP / Swiss) produces the condition-level decomposition of that aggregate.
- The **Delphi-2M 10× asymmetry** is the training-data-gap anchor. It justifies why observed-exposure measurement in healthcare cannot rely on existing Economic Index inputs alone.
